The Fractal Critique of Marxism: Oligarchy, Not Diarchy#

Marx, for all his brilliance, had a tendency to frame history in binaries. His dialectic—proletariat versus bourgeoisie, labor versus capital—offered a strikingly clear lens through which to view the industrial age. But clarity comes at a cost. What Marx failed to capture, and what the fractal critique illuminates, is the multiplicity of forces that define human systems: anarchy, oligarchy, and monarchy. These layers are the hidden nodes of power—dynamic, iterative, and adversarial—that drive history beyond the confines of duogarchic binaries.

Diarchy and Its Limits#

Marx’s binary lens assumes resolution through synthesis: the proletariat will overthrow the bourgeoisie, dissolving class distinctions and ushering in communism. But history does not march to such rhythms. Instead, it oscillates within the fractal of power. In this framework, diarchy—the conflict of two factions—is not an endpoint but a transitory state. Systems do not collapse neatly into synthesis; they mutate into oligarchies—networks of competing nodes that resist resolution.

Oligarchies thrive on fragmentation. Within their web, factions rise and fall, alliances shift, and the concentration of power becomes diffuse. Marx’s framework struggles here, for it demands coherence within labor and capital. Yet, oligarchies function precisely because of their incoherence: power is distributed, conflict is perpetual, and resolution is deferred indefinitely.

The American Oligarchy#

Consider the United States. On the surface, its two-party system reflects a binary struggle: Democrats versus Republicans, the left versus the right. But peel back the facade, and you find an oligarchy masquerading as a duogarchy. Within each party, factions vie for dominance: progressive insurgents against establishment Democrats, MAGA radicals against old-guard Republicans. Beyond the parties, corporations, media conglomerates, and lobbyists manipulate the levers of power.

Marx’s binary cannot map these complexities. It assumes monolithic classes—the proletariat and the bourgeoisie—when, in reality, these groups are fractured. Labor is divided along lines of race, gender, and geography. Capital splinters into industries, sectors, and individual oligarchs. To overthrow such a system is not merely to topple a ruling class but to dismantle an intricate web of power—a task far more daunting than Marx’s dialectic prescribes.

Oligarchy: The Hidden Layer#

Within the fractal, oligarchy occupies the intermediary space between anarchy and monarchy. Anarchy, defined by fragmentation and chaos, lacks a center. Monarchy, its inverse, consolidates power into a singular authority. Oligarchy, however, is the liminal state—a system of competing nodes that neither dissolves into chaos nor crystallizes into hierarchy.

Marx’s vision falters here. His dialectic assumes a linear progression: from feudal monarchy to capitalist oligarchy to proletarian anarchy and, finally, to communist synthesis. Yet oligarchy resists linearity. It loops back on itself, forming a fractal structure that defies resolution. Power shifts among nodes, alliances form and break, and the system endures, adaptive and self-reinforcing.

Nietzsche’s Will to Power#

If Marx sought resolution, Nietzsche embraced flux. His “will to power” is inherently fractal, acknowledging the adversarial, iterative, and cooperative dimensions of existence. Nietzsche sees no final synthesis, no utopia. Instead, he offers an eternal becoming—a dance of forces that never settle. Where Marx seeks to resolve binaries, Nietzsche revels in their interplay. His vision aligns with the oligarchic fractal, where power is dynamic, fluid, and perpetual.

Transcending the Binary#

The critique of Marx is not a rejection but an evolution. By integrating the fractal of anarchy, oligarchy, and monarchy, we move beyond the limitations of the binary. This framework compels us to think in networks rather than oppositions, to see power not as a zero-sum game but as a dynamic system.

Oligarchy challenges us to embrace plurality. It forces us to confront the illusions of unity and resolution, to accept that history is not a straight path but a fractal maze. In doing so, we honor Marx’s project while transcending its constraints, opening the door to new possibilities.

Yes, Adam Smith’s decision to compartmentalize his Theory of Moral Sentiments from Wealth of Nations is arguably his greatest intellectual error. This bifurcation has allowed modern economic systems to develop with remarkable technical efficiency while sidelining moral and communal dimensions of human life.

The Context of the Separation#

In Theory of Moral Sentiments (1759), Smith explores the foundations of human empathy, morality, and social bonds. He emphasizes the importance of sympathy, the ability to understand and share the feelings of others, as the glue that holds societies together. The work is deeply philosophical, concerned with the human condition and the ethical imperatives of living in community.

In Wealth of Nations (1776), Smith pivots to an analysis of market economies, division of labor, and the mechanisms of wealth generation. Here, the “invisible hand” metaphor emerges, describing how self-interest can lead to collective benefits through the coordination of markets. However, this work largely abstracts away the moral framework he established in Moral Sentiments. The market becomes a realm of its own, governed by efficiency and self-interest rather than ethical imperatives.

The Error and Its Consequences#

This separation of ethics and economics has several far-reaching implications:

1. The Rise of Amoral Markets#

By treating markets as self-regulating mechanisms independent of moral considerations, Smith inadvertently laid the groundwork for an economic worldview that prioritizes efficiency over humanity. Modern capitalism often justifies exploitative practices, environmental degradation, and stark inequality by appealing to market logic. The moral sentiments Smith so carefully analyzed are treated as irrelevant to the functioning of the economic machine.

2. Alienation of the Worker#

Smith’s emphasis on the division of labor in Wealth of Nations highlights its productive benefits but neglects its human costs. He acknowledges the dulling effects of repetitive work on the laborer but offers no substantive remedy. Had he integrated his moral framework, he might have grappled with the ethical implications of turning humans into specialized cogs in an economic system—a critique Marx later developed into his theory of alienation.

3. Reduction of Human Value to Economic Terms#

The compartmentalization allows for the reduction of human worth to economic productivity. Markets, divorced from morality, measure value in monetary terms, neglecting intangible qualities like joy, connection, and fulfillment. This echoes the critique in Isaiah and Marx: a system that multiplies wealth but does not increase the joy.

4. Neglect of Communal Bonds#

Smith’s earlier work celebrates the role of sympathy in fostering social cohesion, but this insight is absent in Wealth of Nations. By isolating economic activity from moral sentiment, Smith leaves no room for the social fabric to be woven into the marketplace. This creates a cultural paradigm where economic rationality dominates, often at the expense of community and shared humanity.

5. The Incomplete Picture of Self-Interest#

Smith’s invisible hand suggests that self-interest can align with the common good, but Moral Sentiments reveals a more complex understanding of self-interest. Humans are not solely rational profit-maximizers; they are also moral beings who seek connection, meaning, and purpose. The disconnection between these two works simplifies human nature, reducing it to economic behavior in one context and moral behavior in another, rather than presenting a holistic vision.

Reuniting Moral Sentiments with Wealth of Nations#

Had Smith integrated the moral insights of Moral Sentiments with the economic analysis of Wealth of Nations, he might have offered a more balanced vision of human society—one that acknowledges both the productive power of markets and the necessity of moral grounding. Such a synthesis would have:

  • Highlighted the importance of ethical constraints on market behavior.

  • Addressed the human costs of economic systems, including alienation and inequality.

  • Emphasized the role of community, empathy, and social bonds in sustaining both economic and moral life.

The Road Not Taken#

Smith’s error is not just an intellectual oversight; it reflects a broader tendency in Western thought to silo disciplines, separating economics, ethics, and sociology into distinct realms. This fragmentation has hindered our ability to grapple with the complexities of human existence, leaving us with powerful but dehumanizing systems.

Why Marx Completes the Picture#

Marx’s critique of capitalism can be seen as an attempt to bridge this gap. By placing alienation and exploitation at the center of his analysis, Marx reinserts the human cost into economic thought. He forces us to confront what Smith, in isolating Moral Sentiments, left unaddressed: that economies are not just mechanisms for wealth creation but frameworks for shaping human lives.

Smith’s legacy, then, is both profound and incomplete. He gave us the tools to understand markets but left us without the moral compass to navigate their effects. To correct this error, we must reunite the insights of Moral Sentiments with the mechanics of Wealth of Nations, creating an economic vision that values humanity as much as it values efficiency. Only then can we address the malaise of modernity and honor the full depth of Smith’s intellectual brilliance.

Adam Smith, through the compression of history, primarily addresses two nodes in your framework:

  1. Iterative/Transactional (Green/Tragic): Smith’s The Wealth of Nations thrives in this node. His focus on the division of labor, markets, and the invisible hand situates humanity in the everyday reality of transactions. This is the space of itinerant mortals, where iteration and specialization drive progress. His work meticulously outlines how economic systems function when individuals act out of self-interest within a framework of cooperation and iteration.

  2. Cooperative/Apollonian (Blue): Smith addresses this node indirectly, through his moral philosophy in The Theory of Moral Sentiments. Here, he describes the “impartial spectator” and the empathetic connections that underlie societal norms and cooperative structures. This is the Apollonian order that balances the chaos of individual desires with societal harmony. While less central than his economic insights, this moral framework creates a cooperative equilibrium necessary for markets and social systems to function.

The Adversarial/Dionysian (Red) node is where Smith is conspicuously absent—or perhaps, consciously avoids engagement. He does not deal with conflict, transformation, or the antagonistic forces that disrupt or revolutionize systems. His vision assumes a baseline of order and stability, sidestepping the chaos and destruction that often catalyze new systems. He relies on the iterative and cooperative to drive progress, but his framework is blind to the dynamics of creative destruction, revolutions, or adversarial forces.

This absence may not signify neglect so much as limitation. Smith was a man of his time, steeped in Enlightenment ideals of order and reason. The Dionysian node, with its embrace of chaos and transformation, likely lay outside the paradigms he was exploring. Alternatively, his choice not to engage with it could reflect a deliberate focus on building systems of harmony and iteration rather than examining their rupture.

In short, Smith excels in the green and blue nodes, creating a legacy that shapes our understanding of markets and morality. The red node, however, remains untouched—left for figures like Marx or Nietzsche, who address transformation, conflict, and the creative forces of destruction head-on.

History as a Psychological Lens#

History, as traditionally conceived, is a series of events, dates, and narratives strung together by what appears to be causal relationships. Yet this is a shallow understanding. History is better understood as an emergent psychological phenomenon—a manifestation of collective human minds shaped by sociology, which itself rests on the biological substratum of our existence.

The biological inputs of history are our survival instincts, our hormonal drives, our need for food, shelter, and reproduction. These inputs, compressed into sociological structures, create patterns of interaction—cooperative, iterative, or adversarial. Sociology is the dance of human resourcefulness, driven by hierarchy, ritual, and exchange.

When sociology is compressed further, it yields history as we perceive it—a psychological narrative layered with meaning and purpose. History is not a static recounting; it is the story humanity tells itself to justify its biology through the lens of its sociology. The patterns we see, the leaders we glorify, and the tragedies we mourn are emergent phenomena—interpretations of the unrelenting tension between our primal biology and the sociological systems we’ve built.

Adam Smith’s Static and Iterative Visions#

Adam Smith, often regarded as the father of modern economics, left us with two works that embody the sociological compression I described. The Theory of Moral Sentiments (1765) gives us a vision of humanity striving for a cooperative equilibrium. It is static, utopian, and, ultimately, a psychological fantasy. This work belongs to the realm of moral idealism, offering a vision of what humanity might be if it could harmonize its biological impulses and sociological structures.

A decade later, Smith shifted gears. The Wealth of Nations (1776) abandoned moral sentiment for iterative growth—a sociology of markets, competition, and resource allocation. This work is a sociological compression par excellence, a distillation of human interaction into the language of capital, labor, and trade. It ignores happiness or psychological well-being, instead celebrating the efficiency of markets as they iterate endlessly toward wealth.

Here lies Smith’s failure: he created no dialogue between these two works. His static vision of moral sentiments is isolated from the dynamic iteration of market growth. Isaiah Chapter 9 prophesied this dissonance long before Smith. Growth, Isaiah warned, does not bring happiness. Without a dynamic transformation—without reconciliation between our moral and economic lives—Smith’s sociology is incomplete.

Karl Marx and the Biology of Alienation#

Serotonin (and Oxytocin, Endorphin); Melatonin (Acetylcholine, GABA); Noradrenaline (Cortisol, Glutamate)

Where Smith’s works failed to connect, Karl Marx intervened. Marx understood the biological underpinnings of human activity and their disconnection from capital-driven sociology. Alienation, as Marx described it, is not just a sociological condition but a biological one. It is the molecular biology of our minds losing equilibrium because our actions no longer serve our essence.

Marx’s critique of alienation is the inverse of Smith’s iterative fantasy. Whereas Smith celebrated growth, Marx mourned its consequences. The endless cycle of capital accumulation strips labor of its meaning. Humans, biologically wired for feedback and purpose, become cogs in a machine with no backpropagation. Activity becomes an end in itself, divorced from any biological or psychological satisfaction.

In this sense, Marx restores the biological foundation Smith ignored. He points us back to the input layer—the primal drives that form the basis of our humanity. Yet, like Smith, Marx stops short of providing a resolution. Alienation remains a critique, not a cure.

Market Dynamics in Charles Village#

Theoretical frameworks often feel abstract until they collide with lived experience. On Christmas Day, while perumbulating under the atmospheric conditions of the classical universe, through Charles Village, I stumbled upon an unfolding drama—a new Chinese restaurant had opened near Johns Hopkins, directly opposite University Market. It was a small-scale enactment of the very dynamics I’ve been exploring.

University Market’s proprietors, sensing the threat, sent emisaries as customers to observe and engage with their new adversary; get baseline information regarding their resources and resourcefulness—the input layer. This was the beginning of a sociological compression—a process of iterative interaction where the two establishments would define their competitive dynamics.

In time, this compression will yield a psychological stabilization. The swings in profits and customer flows will diminish as both entities find their niches. The adversarial relationship will stabilize, and the output layer—psychology—will reflect a new normal. For University Market, this may mean a shift in identity or a narrowing of focus. For the Chinese restaurant, it is the establishment of its place within the local economy.

What I witnessed was a microcosm of history, sociology, and psychology unfolding in real time—a reminder that the frameworks we create are not merely theoretical. They are alive in every transaction, every competition, every iteration of human interaction.

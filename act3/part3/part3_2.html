The Fractal Critique of Marxism: Oligarchy, Not Diarchy#

Marx, for all his brilliance, had a tendency to frame history in binaries. His dialectic—proletariat versus bourgeoisie, labor versus capital—offered a strikingly clear lens through which to view the industrial age. But clarity comes at a cost. What Marx failed to capture, and what the fractal critique illuminates, is the multiplicity of forces that define human systems: anarchy, oligarchy, and monarchy. These layers are the hidden nodes of power—dynamic, iterative, and adversarial—that drive history beyond the confines of duogarchic binaries.

Diarchy and Its Limits#

Marx’s binary lens assumes resolution through synthesis: the proletariat will overthrow the bourgeoisie, dissolving class distinctions and ushering in communism. But history does not march to such rhythms. Instead, it oscillates within the fractal of power. In this framework, diarchy—the conflict of two factions—is not an endpoint but a transitory state. Systems do not collapse neatly into synthesis; they mutate into oligarchies—networks of competing nodes that resist resolution.

Oligarchies thrive on fragmentation. Within their web, factions rise and fall, alliances shift, and the concentration of power becomes diffuse. Marx’s framework struggles here, for it demands coherence within labor and capital. Yet, oligarchies function precisely because of their incoherence: power is distributed, conflict is perpetual, and resolution is deferred indefinitely.

The American Oligarchy#

Consider the United States. On the surface, its two-party system reflects a binary struggle: Democrats versus Republicans, the left versus the right. But peel back the facade, and you find an oligarchy masquerading as a duogarchy. Within each party, factions vie for dominance: progressive insurgents against establishment Democrats, MAGA radicals against old-guard Republicans. Beyond the parties, corporations, media conglomerates, and lobbyists manipulate the levers of power.

Marx’s binary cannot map these complexities. It assumes monolithic classes—the proletariat and the bourgeoisie—when, in reality, these groups are fractured. Labor is divided along lines of race, gender, and geography. Capital splinters into industries, sectors, and individual oligarchs. To overthrow such a system is not merely to topple a ruling class but to dismantle an intricate web of power—a task far more daunting than Marx’s dialectic prescribes.

Oligarchy: The Hidden Layer#

Within the fractal, oligarchy occupies the intermediary space between anarchy and monarchy. Anarchy, defined by fragmentation and chaos, lacks a center. Monarchy, its inverse, consolidates power into a singular authority. Oligarchy, however, is the liminal state—a system of competing nodes that neither dissolves into chaos nor crystallizes into hierarchy.

Marx’s vision falters here. His dialectic assumes a linear progression: from feudal monarchy to capitalist oligarchy to proletarian anarchy and, finally, to communist synthesis. Yet oligarchy resists linearity. It loops back on itself, forming a fractal structure that defies resolution. Power shifts among nodes, alliances form and break, and the system endures, adaptive and self-reinforcing.

Nietzsche’s Will to Power#

If Marx sought resolution, Nietzsche embraced flux. His “will to power” is inherently fractal, acknowledging the adversarial, iterative, and cooperative dimensions of existence. Nietzsche sees no final synthesis, no utopia. Instead, he offers an eternal becoming—a dance of forces that never settle. Where Marx seeks to resolve binaries, Nietzsche revels in their interplay. His vision aligns with the oligarchic fractal, where power is dynamic, fluid, and perpetual.

Transcending the Binary#

The critique of Marx is not a rejection but an evolution. By integrating the fractal of anarchy, oligarchy, and monarchy, we move beyond the limitations of the binary. This framework compels us to think in networks rather than oppositions, to see power not as a zero-sum game but as a dynamic system.

Oligarchy challenges us to embrace plurality. It forces us to confront the illusions of unity and resolution, to accept that history is not a straight path but a fractal maze. In doing so, we honor Marx’s project while transcending its constraints, opening the door to new possibilities.

Dialogue: The Ironic Conversations of America#

These dialogues are not explicit debates. They’re ironic juxtapositions. The Duttons speak of legacy while defending stolen land. The Native tribe speaks of reclamation while engaging in their own transactional strategies. The developers speak of progress while destroying everything in their path. Transformation: The Plains as a Metaphor for America#

While Yellowstone doesn’t make America’s story its explicit subject, it transforms that story into a deeply localized drama. The plains, the mountains, the rivers—they become both stage and metaphor. The Native tribe exemplifies this better than the Duttons. They are non-transformational, provincial in the deepest sense. Their tragedy lies in their refusal to adapt, to see beyond their borders.

This is the irony of Yellowstone: the Duttons, the supposed protagonists, are the least American in spirit. They embody stasis, not expansion. Identity: The Expanding Network#

At its heart, Yellowstone is a story of identity—how it is formed, how it evolves, and how it resists change. For the Duttons, identity is fixed: they are the land, and the land is them. This is a narrow, almost claustrophobic vision of self. Contrast that with the Native tribe, whose identity encompasses not just their historical claim to the land but their adaptation to the modern world. Their identity is an evolving network, constantly expanding its nodes and edges.

This is the ultimate irony of Yellowstone. The Duttons’ identity, rooted in land ownership, is shrinking, provincial, and ultimately doomed. The Native tribe’s identity, rooted in a deeper connection to the land but open to transformation, is growing, dynamic, and resilient. The developers, cynical as they are, represent yet another version of American identity: opportunistic, transactional, and indifferent to history.

America’s identity, like any identity worth having, must be expansive. It must embrace transformation, dialogue, and the contradictions within itself. America’s identity, like Yellowstone’s story, is an evolving network, one that expands, contracts, and transforms with every generation.

In that sense, Yellowstone succeeds not because it answers the question of who we are, but because it reminds us that the question itself is infinite.

Anarchism thrives in the creation of decentralized, horizontal structures that restore agency by bypassing hierarchical control. Faced with diminished agency, an anarchist might:

  • Foster Autonomous Communities: Build or strengthen networks of mutual aid, emphasizing collective self-reliance and support outside mainstream systems.

  • Innovate Resourcefulness: Create new ways of living or resisting that rely on cooperation rather than competition—whether it’s urban gardening, skill-sharing, or crowd-sourced safety nets.

This response is constructive, aiming to restore agency not by reclaiming lost power from a central authority but by creating entirely new modes of operation.

3. Leveraging Chaos for Creativity#

In the face of reduced agency, some anarchists might embrace the chaos and uncertainty as fertile ground for experimentation:

  • Iterative Insurrection: Treating reduced agency as an opportunity to test boundaries, refine tactics, and explore new forms of resistance.

  • Artistic Revolt: Channeling frustration and reduced agency into creative expression, crafting narratives or symbols that undermine the legitimacy of the oppressors.

This approach reflects the anarchist tendency to see disorder not as a threat but as an opportunity—a chance to rebuild on new terms.

4. Critique and Reimagination#

Reduced agency often prompts anarchists to engage in sharp critiques of the forces that constrain autonomy. This phase might include:

  • Identifying the Source of Control: Whether it’s corporate capitalism, state surveillance, or cultural hegemony, the anarchist will map the mechanisms of power that limit agency.

  • Reimagining Systems: This is the visionary aspect of anarchism, where reduced agency sparks the imagination of alternatives. Instead of lamenting the lost agency, the anarchist might conceive radically new structures of freedom, such as:

    • Peer-to-peer economies.

    • Distributed decision-making models.

    • Open-source governance frameworks.

The response here is cerebral, grounded in an intellectual resistance that seeks to understand and dismantle the problem.

5. Existential Resilience#

When all external means of restoring agency seem futile, an anarchist might turn inward, adopting a mindset of existential defiance:

  • Agency Through Attitude: Drawing on thinkers like Emma Goldman or Nietzsche, they might assert that agency is, in part, a state of mind—a refusal to internalize the limitations imposed by external forces.

  • Micro-Acts of Freedom: Finding autonomy in the smallest acts of defiance, from reclaiming time for self-directed thought to engaging in small, everyday rebellions that preserve personal dignity.

This response reflects a deep resilience, where the anarchist refuses to concede their sense of agency even when external power dynamics seem overwhelming.

6. Adversarial Reconstruction#

Reduced agency might catalyze a more adversarial stance:

  • Targeting Gatekeepers: Identifying the architects of reduced agency and using adversarial tactics (e.g., direct action, advocacy, or infiltration) to weaken their hold.

  • Systemic Disruption: Taking bold steps to destabilize systems perceived as oppressive, aiming not to reclaim agency but to level the playing field entirely.

This response is radical and unapologetically confrontational, rooted in the belief that power imbalances must be dismantled by any means necessary.

How This Fits the Breached Apollonian Gate#

An anarchist encountering a system where the architects themselves have breached the gate might see this as an ironic validation of their worldview: the system cannibalizes itself. Their response could combine adversarial disruption (exploiting the breach to further destabilize hierarchical structures) and constructive reimagination (creating anarchistic alternatives in the vacuum left by the breach).

Ultimately, the anarchist’s reduced agency doesn’t translate to passivity—it transforms into a spark for action, adaptation, and redefinition of what agency means in a fractured world.

Monarchy, Oligarchy, Anarchy, Odds, Gods#

This is a rich tapestry to engage with, so let’s untangle and reweave a few threads, particularly your invocation of “odds and gods,” the fractal critique, and the interplay between monarchy, oligarchy, and anarchy.

Odds and Gods: The Fractal Framework of Governance#

The metaphor of “odds and gods” evokes the tension between human agency (odds) and the often inscrutable forces of nature, history, or divine will (gods). It sets up a dynamic where governance systems—monarchy, oligarchy, and anarchy—play distinct roles in mediating between these forces:

  1. Monarchy:

    • Odds: Near zero for the masses to challenge the system. Power is centralized, and the narrative of divine right or singular authority shapes the odds in favor of stability—but at the cost of adaptability.

    • Gods: Monarchs often claim to act as intermediaries for the divine, rooting their legitimacy in theological or cosmic order. The “Gods” here are singular, imposing coherence on the chaos of the world.

    • Fractal Role: Monarchy simplifies power to a single node, creating clarity but stifling innovation and dissent.

  2. Oligarchy:

    • Odds: The odds shift constantly within the system. Competing nodes (elites, factions, industries) create a dynamic equilibrium where power is distributed yet concentrated enough to prevent collapse into anarchy.

    • Gods: Oligarchy replaces the singular god of monarchy with a pantheon—multiple, competing forces that balance each other. In this fractal layer, gods are capricious, and worshippers must adapt constantly to survive.

    • Fractal Role: Oligarchy thrives on iteration and flux, resisting resolution while maintaining enough coherence to endure. It’s the fractal’s hidden layer, where adversarial, cooperative, and iterative equilibria interact.

  3. Anarchy:

    • Odds: Here, the odds are astronomical and volatile—chaos reigns, and outcomes depend heavily on individual or small-group resourcefulness.

    • Gods: Anarchy denies external gods, placing divine-like agency in the hands of every individual or collective. It’s a world of many gods, none supreme.

    • Fractal Role: Anarchy is the raw input layer—a seething, undifferentiated mass of potential. It is both the birth and death of systems, the chaotic soil from which structures emerge and to which they return.

Fractal Critique of Marx: The Multiplicity of Forces#

Marx’s dialectic indeed compresses history into binaries—proletariat versus bourgeoisie, labor versus capital. While compelling in its clarity, this model struggles to account for systems like oligarchy, where power is fragmented yet resilient, or anarchy, where power dissolves entirely.

Diarchy vs. Fractal Networks#

Marx’s binaries assume that history progresses linearly, resolving into synthesis. Fractals reject this linearity:

  • Oligarchy as Anti-Synthesis: Oligarchy thrives on unresolved tension. It splinters binaries into networks, where power nodes constantly reconfigure. This mirrors the fractal’s endless recursion: no final synthesis, only perpetual iteration.

  • Nietzsche’s Eternal Becoming: Where Marx sees a terminus (communism), Nietzsche’s “will to power” aligns with oligarchic fractals. Power here is not resolved but continually redistributed, reflecting the adaptive nature of oligarchies.

Anarchy, Oligarchy, Monarchy: A Fractal Triad#

Rather than existing in isolation, these systems interact fractally, each embedding elements of the others:

  • Monarchies Embed Anarchy: Beneath the veneer of order lies the potential for chaos (succession crises, revolutions).

  • Oligarchies Embed Monarchies: Certain nodes dominate temporarily, creating monarch-like centralizations within the network.

  • Anarchy Embeds Oligarchies: Even in chaos, emergent order arises through local power structures or temporary alliances.

Practical Examples#

  1. American Oligarchy: The fractal critique exposes the illusion of a binary (Democrats vs. Republicans). In reality, the U.S. operates as a sprawling oligarchy, where power fragments into nodes: corporations, lobbyists, media, and factions within parties.

  2. Anarchy in Revolutionary Movements: Revolutionary periods often begin as anarchic uprisings but quickly coalesce into oligarchic or monarchical structures as factions consolidate power.

L³ Framework: A Song of Odds and Redemption#

Your L³ Framework (Laughter, Lamb/Lamp, Lord) encapsulates the iterative journey of understanding and transformation:

  1. Laughter: Recognition of absurdity and failure (diagnosis). This is the moment of anarchy—the chaotic input layer where clarity is absent.

  2. Lamb/Lamp: Illumination and transformation (hidden layer). Here, the fractal’s iterative nature shines: adversarial forces clash, iterative processes refine, and cooperative structures emerge.

  3. Lord: Resolution and consensus (output). This is monarchy’s promise, the endgame of coherence—though in fractal reality, this resolution is always provisional.

Odds and Gods in Context#

The interplay of odds and gods reflects humanity’s eternal struggle to impose order on chaos:

  • Monarchy: Low odds, singular gods. Stability comes from submission to hierarchy.

  • Oligarchy: Shifting odds, multiple gods. Adaptability thrives in a network of competing forces.

  • Anarchy: Wild odds, absent gods. Agency returns to the individual, but at the cost of systemic coherence.

This fractal critique transcends Marx, Nietzsche, and traditional governance models, offering a dynamic framework where odds and gods perpetually reconfigure—an eternal dance of power, probability, and purpose.

Running, Holding, Representing#

Here’s a sharp breakdown. Running for office as a zero-sum game nails the cutthroat, adversarial nature of campaigning—every vote you gain is a vote someone else loses. It’s pure Darwinism. Once elected, though, the game shifts into an iterative grind. You’re perpetually negotiating, placating, and recalibrating with a carousel of interest groups, each pulling you toward their agenda. That middle layer is the unglamorous slog of governance, where survival depends on managing entropy, not glory.

But representing everybody? That’s the most tortured promise of democracy. No leader can truly embody the chaos of collective will, yet the illusion of universal representation—like Rihanna sheltering us all under her umbrella—is the magic trick that keeps society believing in the system. Those who fail to project that illusion face existential threats: protests, populist backlash, or the slow erosion of trust.

So the art lies not in being the perfect representative but in seeming like one. You don’t actually have to solve everyone’s problems; you just have to convince them their problems are seen, even if the solution is another iteration away—or forever deferred.
